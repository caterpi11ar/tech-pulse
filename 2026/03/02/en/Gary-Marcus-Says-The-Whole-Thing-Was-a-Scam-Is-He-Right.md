# Gary Marcus Says "The Whole Thing Was a Scam" — Is He Right?

AI's most persistent critic just published his strongest statement yet. Let's actually engage with it.

## What Happened

Gary Marcus published "The whole thing was a scam" on Substack, offering a systematic critique of the current AI boom. The post hit Hacker News with genuine debate in the comments — rare in a space where people usually just agree or ignore.

Marcus is a cognitive scientist at NYU and has been one of the most consistent critical voices in AI since at least 2022.

## Why It Matters

Marcus's critiques deserve engagement, not dismissal. His core arguments have been consistent: LLMs are statistical pattern matchers, not reasoners; the current scaling trajectory has limits; AI company valuations are built on systematic capability exaggeration.

These claims were widely mocked in 2022-2023. But with GPT-4's release now two years in the rearview mirror and no clear successor in sight, with multiple "AGI by 2025" predictions quietly retired — some of his warnings look more credible than they did.

## My Take

Marcus's problem isn't that he's wrong. It's that he consistently uses the strongest possible framing for moderately strong claims. "The whole thing was a scam" and "current AI capabilities are overstated" are very different propositions. He packages the second as the first, which makes him a great traffic generator but a less credible serious critic.

Still, the underlying question is worth sitting with: if AI companies don't deliver meaningful capability jumps in the next 18 months, what happens to the investment thesis? Not just valuations — the entire narrative infrastructure that sustains the current level of enterprise adoption.

That's a real question. Marcus is asking it louder than most.

## What to Watch

- GPT-5 / Claude 5 capability improvements will directly test Marcus's ceiling hypothesis
- Will AI company IPO timelines shift if capability growth visibly plateaus?
- How should the field rethink LLM evaluation if current benchmarks have saturated?

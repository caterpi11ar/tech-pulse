# OpenAI Published Its Own Military Deal Statement — Here's What It Actually Says

When a company writes its own defense, the gaps are often more revealing than the words.

![OpenAI statement](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/6889473510b50328dbb70ae6/68c469d1859e3e7ecf6c4c95_og-default.png)

## What Happened

OpenAI published a statement titled "Our Agreement with the Department of War" on its official website, formally confirming a partnership with the U.S. Department of Defense. This marks the first time OpenAI has addressed the controversy in its own voice, after days of user backlash and the viral "Cancel ChatGPT" movement.

The post hit Hacker News and triggered immediate debate — not just about the deal itself, but about the framing.

## Why It Matters

The fact that OpenAI felt compelled to publish this statement tells you something: the cost of silence exceeded the cost of explanation.

The content is carefully worded. OpenAI draws a line between "defensive applications" and "offensive weapons," arguing their technology will only be used for the former. But in modern warfare, this distinction is largely theoretical. Drone guidance systems, target identification, logistics optimization — all could be classified either way depending on who's asking.

The statement also notably uses "Department of War" — the pre-1947 name for the DoD. Whether that's intentional irony or a PR misstep is genuinely unclear.

## My Take

This is a failed piece of crisis communication. It acknowledges the fact but provides no convincing moral framework. "We only help with defense, not offense" is not a meaningful ethical boundary in 21st-century warfare — military ethicists have known this for decades.

More fundamentally: OpenAI is a private company. There is no democratic mechanism to constrain its decisions about which military uses are acceptable. That judgment now rests entirely with Sam Altman and a board that was recently reorganized under significant controversy.

## What to Watch

- Will OpenAI's board demand more transparency around government contracts?
- Will Google DeepMind or other labs follow suit — or publicly distance themselves?
- Could this trigger congressional scrutiny of AI military applications?
